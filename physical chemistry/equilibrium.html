Chemical Equilibrium

Chemical Equilibrium is a state in which the forward and reverse reactions occur at equal rates, leading to stable concentrations of reactants and products. This state does not imply the cessation of reactions but rather a dynamic balance between them.

Fundamental Concepts in Chemical Equilibrium

1. Dynamic Equilibrium

In a closed system, reactions can reach dynamic equilibrium, where the rate of the forward reaction equals the rate of the reverse reaction. At equilibrium, reactant and product concentrations remain constant but not necessarily equal.

2. Equilibrium Constant (K)

The equilibrium constant (K) expresses the ratio of product concentrations to reactant concentrations at equilibrium, raised to the power of their stoichiometric coefficients. For a general reaction:

aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD

The equilibrium constant is given by:

K = [C]^c [D]^d / [A]^a [B]^b

Where [C], [D], [A], and [B] are the molar concentrations of the substances. K depends on temperature and is different for each reaction.

3. Types of Equilibrium Constants

  • Kc: Equilibrium constant in terms of concentration (mol/L).
  • Kp: Equilibrium constant in terms of partial pressure, used for gas-phase reactions.
  • Ksp: Solubility product constant, relevant for sparingly soluble salts in water.
  • Ka and Kb: Equilibrium constants for acid and base ionization reactions.

4. Reaction Quotient (Q)

The reaction quotient (Q) is similar to K but for non-equilibrium conditions. Comparing Q with K predicts the direction of the reaction:

  • If Q < K: The reaction proceeds forward to reach equilibrium.
  • If Q = K: The system is at equilibrium.
  • If Q > K: The reaction proceeds in the reverse direction.

5. Le Chatelier's Principle

Le Chatelier's Principle states that if a dynamic equilibrium is disturbed, the system will adjust itself to counteract the disturbance and re-establish equilibrium. This applies to changes in concentration, pressure, volume, and temperature.

  • Concentration: Adding more reactants or products shifts equilibrium to restore balance.
  • Pressure: Increasing pressure favors the side with fewer gas molecules.
  • Temperature: Heating favors the endothermic direction, while cooling favors the exothermic direction.

6. Gibbs Free Energy and Equilibrium

The relationship between Gibbs free energy (ΔG) and equilibrium determines reaction spontaneity:

ΔG = ΔG° + RT lnQ

At equilibrium, ΔG = 0, so:

ΔG° = -RT lnK

If ΔG° < 0, K > 1, favoring products; if ΔG° > 0, K < 1, favoring reactants.

Applications of Chemical Equilibrium

Chemical equilibrium principles are applied in various industrial and environmental processes, such as:

  • Haber Process: Production of ammonia (NH3) using N2 and H2 under optimized pressure and temperature.
  • Contact Process: Manufacturing of sulfuric acid (H2SO4) using SO2 and O2.
  • Solubility Control: Understanding precipitation reactions in water treatment and metal recovery.
  • Biological Systems: Equilibrium concepts in metabolic reactions and oxygen transport in the blood.

Important Equilibrium Equations

  • Equilibrium Constant (K): K = [products] / [reactants]
  • Gibbs Free Energy and Equilibrium: ΔG° = -RT lnK
  • Reaction Quotient (Q): Q = [C]^c [D]^d / [A]^a [B]^b

Practical Implications of Chemical Equilibrium

Chemical equilibrium principles help optimize industrial processes and understand environmental and biological systems. Techniques like the Haber process depend on carefully adjusting conditions to maximize product yield, while equilibrium in natural systems explains phenomena like gas exchange in respiration.
